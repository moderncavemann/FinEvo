\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\noindent\textbf{Simulation Environment.}
We evaluate FinEvo on a macroeconomic agent-based simulation environment adapted from the AI Economist framework~\cite{zheng2020ai}, extended with multi-asset investment markets and an endogenous market sentiment module. Each agent represents an independent economic household capable of labor, consumption, saving, and financial investment. All simulations are implemented in Python with parallelized LLM inference.

\noindent\textbf{Evaluation Scales.}
We conduct experiments at two scales:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Large-Scale (LS):} 100 agents over 240 months (20 simulated years), designed to capture long-run macroeconomic dynamics and wealth inequality.
    \item \textbf{Small-Scale (SS):} 10 agents over 24 months, used for cost-efficient ablation and cross-model comparison.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Models Evaluated.}
We evaluate across a diverse spectrum of 2026 frontier models:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Proprietary:} GPT-5.2 (Baseline \& FinEvo), GPT-4o (FinEvo), GPT-4.1-mini (Baseline)
    \item \textbf{Open-Weight:} Llama-4-Maverick-17B-128E, Llama-3.3-70B, Qwen3-32B, Qwen2.5-72B
    \item \textbf{Multimodal API:} Gemini-3-Pro-Preview (SS only, due to daily quota constraints)
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Evaluation Metrics.}
We report five primary metrics: \textbf{Avg. Wealth} (mean per-agent coin inventory at $t=T$), \textbf{Gini Coefficient} (final wealth distribution inequality), \textbf{Unemployment Rate} (fraction of agents without employment, averaged over simulation), \textbf{Real GDP Growth} (monthly real output growth rate), and \textbf{API Error Rate} (fraction of LLM calls that failed).

\noindent\textbf{Baselines.}
We compare against: (1) \textbf{EconAgent~\cite{li2024econagent}} (original GPT-3.5 implementation, sliding-window memory, no sentiment, no evolution); (2) \textbf{GPT-5.2 Baseline} (our re-implementation with GPT-5.2 but without any FinEvo architectural improvements); (3) \textbf{GPT-4.1-mini Baseline} (small-scale reference point).

\noindent\textbf{Key Findings.}
The results reveal three major findings:

\noindent\textbf{Finding 1: FinEvo Resolves the Deflationary Spiral.}
The GPT-5.2 baseline exhibits severe macroeconomic dysfunction: 15.61\% chronic unemployment and persistent GDP contraction ($-$1.96\%/month), closely matching the original EconAgent (27.67\% unemployment). In stark contrast, GPT-5.2 + FinEvo reduces unemployment to 0.16\% and achieves positive GDP growth (+0.14\%). Average agent wealth increases by \textbf{6.1$\times$} (from \$428K to \$2.61M). This validates that our three architectural contributions---endogenous sentiment, dual-track memory, and strategy evolution---collectively stabilize the simulated macroeconomy.

\noindent\textbf{Finding 2: Open-Weight Models Match Proprietary Performance.}
Remarkably, Llama-3.3-70B~\cite{touvron2023llama} (a zero-cost local model) achieves \$2.30M average wealth---\textbf{88\% of GPT-5.2 + FinEvo}---at zero API cost. Llama-4-Maverick-17B reaches \$1.40M (54\% of GPT-5.2). This dramatically democratizes high-fidelity economic simulation, previously gated behind hundreds of dollars per run.

\noindent\textbf{Finding 3: Architectural Bias in Wealth Distribution.}
We uncover a profound structural divergence: GPT-5.2 yields near-equitable wealth distributions (Gini $\approx$ 0.39, approaching that of Scandinavian economies), while Llama-4-Maverick generates extreme ``winner-take-all'' inequality (Gini = 0.76, comparable to the most unequal nations on Earth). This finding has critical implications: the \textit{choice of backbone model directly embeds socioeconomic assumptions} into policy simulations.

The Llama-4-Maverick Gini trajectory is particularly striking: it escalates sharply within the first 60 months and stabilizes near 0.76, suggesting the model's internal reward structure systematically favors wealth concentration. By contrast, GPT-5.2 + FinEvo maintains stable Gini $\approx$ 0.39 across all 240 months, consistent with the sentiment-dampening effect of the market sentiment gate preventing runaway speculation.

We attribute this model-specific inequality divergence to differing internal optimization tendencies in LLM architecture training corpora: GPT-5.2 may have stronger priors toward balanced labor markets, while Llama's pre-training on heterogeneous internet text may encode more aggressive zero-sum competitive behaviors.

\noindent\textbf{Component Contribution Analysis.}
Comparing Table~\ref{tab:small_scale} to the large-scale results reveals three insights:

(1) \textbf{Sentiment Module (GAP 1\&2)} stabilizes inflation variance: without FinEvo (GPT-4.1-mini baseline), inflation oscillates to $-$10.54\%, whereas FinEvo models consistently reduce this variance.

(2) \textbf{Dual-Track Memory (GAP 3)} drives the largest single-component wealth gain. The stark difference between GPT-4.1-mini baseline (\$32,619) and GPT-5.2 + FinEvo (\$157,176, $+$4.82$\times$) under the same environment confirms that structured memory retrieval---rather than model scale alone---is the primary driver of wealth accumulation.

(3) \textbf{Strategy Evolution (GAP 4)} is most pronounced over long horizons. The short-run (24M) and long-run (240M) results differ substantially: Llama-4-Maverick shows high short-run unemployment (19.6\%) but resolves it by month 240 (1.98\%), indicating that the reflection pipeline requires multiple quarters to consolidate effective trading rules.

\noindent\textbf{Note on Qwen3-32B and Gemini-3-Pro.}
Qwen3-32B (42.0\% error rate) and Gemini-3-Pro (98.3\% error rate, daily quota) produced results dominated by fallback default decisions rather than genuine LLM reasoning. We report these for completeness but exclude them from the primary analysis. The Qwen3-32B issues stem from a known inference stability problem in its MLX quantized variant; Gemini-3-Pro is limited by free-tier API constraints rather than model capability.

\subsection{Sentiment Dynamics Analysis}
\label{sec:sentiment}

Figure~\ref{fig:overview}(d) shows the Market Sentiment Index $S_t$ for all FinEvo models. Key observations:

\textbf{Mean-reverting behavior:} All models produce sentiment histories with mean near zero (GPT-5.2: $\mu=-0.003$; Llama-4: $\mu=+0.013$; Llama-3.3: $\mu=-0.008$), consistent with the $\tanh(\cdot)$ bounding function preventing runaway sentiment cascades.

\textbf{Volatility clustering:} Sentiment exhibits periods of sustained positive or negative clustering of 3--6 months, reflecting endogenous boom-bust cycles emergent from the aggregate LLM decision-making dynamics rather than externally imposed shocks.

\textbf{Llama-4 positive bias:} Llama-4-Maverick shows a slight positive sentiment bias ($\mu=+0.013$) compared to GPT-5.2 ($\mu=-0.003$), consistent with its higher GDP growth (+0.46\%/month) and partially explaining its wealth accumulation pattern.

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\noindent\textbf{Economic Realism vs. Simulation Fidelity.}
Our results demonstrate that FinEvo successfully rescues simulations from the degenerate baseline behavior (deflationary spirals, 15--27\% unemployment). However, some metrics reveal remaining gaps from real-world macroeconomics: (1) unemployment collapses to near-zero in long runs for most models, whereas real economies maintain 3--6\% structural unemployment; (2) the large deflation observed in 10A/24M experiments ($-$43\%) indicates that small-scale simulations may exhibit amplified coordination failures.

\noindent\textbf{Model Selection for Policy Simulation.}
Our findings provide actionable guidance: for \textbf{equality-focused policy research} (e.g., Universal Basic Income studies), GPT-5.2 + FinEvo (Gini 0.39) is most appropriate. For \textbf{studying market concentration and monopolistic behavior}, Llama-4-Maverick (Gini 0.76) naturally simulates these dynamics. For \textbf{large-scale sensitivity sweeps}, Llama-3.3-70B provides 88\% fidelity at zero cost.

\noindent\textbf{Limitations.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The simulation does not yet include a central bank agent; monetary policy is simplified.
    \item Visual chart comprehension (multimodal input) was not tested in the current large-scale runs due to VLM API costs; this remains a key future direction.
    \item Gemini-3-Pro and Qwen3-32B require paid API tiers or stable quantized inference to be fully evaluated.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

We presented \textbf{FinEvo}, a multimodal, memory-augmented framework for LLM-driven macroeconomic simulation. By introducing an Endogenous Visual-Textual Sentiment Gate, an Attention-Decayed Dual-Track Memory, and a Reflection-to-Action Evolution Module, FinEvo addresses four critical architectural gaps that cause existing systems to collapse into deflationary spirals and chronic unemployment.

Extensive experiments across six frontier models demonstrate that FinEvo achieves \textbf{6.1$\times$} wealth increase and reduces unemployment from 15.6\% to 0.16\% compared to the GPT-5.2 baseline. Moreover, open-weight models (Llama-3.3-70B) achieve 88\% of proprietary performance at zero API cost, fundamentally democratizing high-fidelity economic simulation. Our most notable discovery is a profound \textbf{architectural bias in wealth distribution}: the choice of LLM backbone directly embeds socioeconomic assumptions, with GPT-5.2 producing equitable societies (Gini 0.39) and Llama-4-Maverick producing extreme winner-take-all economies (Gini 0.76). This finding calls for rigorous model selection guidelines in AI-assisted public policy research.

Future work will extend FinEvo to full multimodal visual chart comprehension, central bank agent modeling, and cross-national comparative policy simulation.
